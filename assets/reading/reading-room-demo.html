The Reading Room

A walkthrough of the premium reading furniture: a progress bar that tracks the climb, a read-time estimate measured from the prose, a chapter rail that lights the section you are in, and citation cards that open on hover or focus. Everything below is rendered by one stylesheet and one script — turn the script off and the page still reads.

The progress bar

At the very top of the window sits a hairline track. As you scroll, an electric-blue fill advances across it — the charge, glowing softly per the house's Blue Law. It is a progressbar in the accessibility tree too, announcing the percentage to assistive technology. The fill reaches one hundred percent exactly when the end of the article reaches the bottom of your viewport, so the bar means what it says: full means you have seen the end.

This first reference is harvested automatically from the roots block at the foot of the page — hover or tab to the black hole entry and a card appears with the citation as it was written below.

The read-time estimate

Just under the dek you will find a small chip: N min read. It is computed at load time from the article's word count, divided by an adult prose rate of two hundred twenty-five words per minute, with the navigation furniture, the roots block, and any code excluded from the count. No server, no build step — the number is honest to the words actually on the page.

Why a clone is counted

To avoid counting its own injected chrome, the script clones the article, strips the rail and the roots, and counts the remainder. The page you read is never touched. This keeps the estimate stable even though the component adds a rail and a chip of its own after load.

The chapter rail

On a wide screen, a rail floats in the left gutter, built from every h2 in the article. Click any entry to glide to that section; the current one stays lit in gold with a small charged tick. On a narrow screen the rail folds into a "Contents" disclosure at the top instead, so it never crowds the column. This is a real navigation landmark with in-page links — usable by keyboard and screen reader alike.

Citations can also be configured explicitly. This link to Ramanujan's notebooks draws its card from an inline JSON sources block rather than from the roots — proof the two paths coexist.

The citation cards

Any link marked as a reference grows a faint gold underline and a small superscript cue. On hover — or on keyboard focus — a preview card opens near the link, Wikipedia-preview style: a kicker, a title, optional bibliographic detail, a short blurb, and a button through to the source. Pressing Escape dismisses it; moving away dismisses it; the card itself stays alive while your pointer is over it so the link inside stays clickable.

One more, inline-configured, with a full blurb: the Kerr metric — the exact-rotating-black-hole solution the house spent a week rendering.

Graceful by default

Every feature is additive. With JavaScript disabled, you get the article exactly as authored — heading anchors, roots links, prose. With the script on, the furniture appears without a single change to the markup. That is the whole contract: progressive enhancement, theme-native, and quiet until invited.

Knowledge is free — forever. The furniture only helps you carry it.
