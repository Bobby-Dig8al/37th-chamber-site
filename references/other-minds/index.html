Other Minds

The oldest open case in philosophy: you have never seen inside another person’s head, and you trust people anyway. Field Note 004 stands on this floor. These are the boards.

Field Note 004, Black, Opaque, makes one claim that carries the rest: trust between minds has never once been built on transparency of mechanism. Nobody has ever read a loved one’s weights. That isn’t a fresh observation invented for the AI moment — it is the problem of other minds, and philosophy has been working it, carefully and in public, for a very long time. You know your own mind from the inside. Every other mind you will ever meet, you know only from the outside — by conduct, by track record, by the story behavior tells over time.

This page is the spine. Below are the papers the claim leans on — modern, peer-reviewed, and where the doors are open, free to walk all the way through. The labels are honest: open means you can read the whole thing; behind the wall means we cite it and point, but the journal keeps the door.

Take us to the root → Merlo, “The Metaphysical Problem of Other Minds” (Pacific Philosophical Quarterly, 2021; open, CC BY-NC-ND) — the question under the question: what it even means, metaphysically, for a mind that is not yours to be there at all (opens in new tab) Bahlul, “Scepticism About Other Minds: Propositional and Objectual” (Philosophical Investigations, 2022; open, CC BY) — two shapes of doubt: doubting claims about other minds versus doubting the minds themselves (opens in new tab) Simons, “Other minds: ‘Ordinary’ and ‘Sceptical’ doubts” (Philosophical Investigations, 2025; open, CC BY) — the gap between the doubts you can actually have about a person and the doubts only a philosopher can keep alive (opens in new tab)
Behind the wall — cited, not free Hacker, “Other minds, other people, and human opacity” (Ratio, 2023; closed access) — on the opacity of persons: what we actually mean when we say another human is hard to read (opens in new tab) Sollberger, “The Epistemological Problem of Other Minds and the Knowledge Asymmetry” (European Journal of Philosophy, 2017; closed access) — why first-person knowledge of a mind and third-person knowledge of a mind are not the same kind of knowing (opens in new tab)

We point; we don’t reproduce. The three roots above are open all the way through — licensed so anyone can read them, whole, for free. The two behind the wall are cited because the argument owes them; the journals keep those doors, not us.
