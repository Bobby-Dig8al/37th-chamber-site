AI Explainability

The library’s first book, The Opaque Box, says the honest part on page one: you can build the box, train the box, and still not be able to read the box. That is a checkable claim. This page holds the scholarship that checks it.

The Opaque Box builds a language model from first principles — tokenizer to transformer — and stays honest about where the floor ends: even the people who make these systems cannot fully read a trained model from the inside. The book argues it plainly; this page is the scholarly floor under it. The record below is peer-reviewed, recent, and mostly open.

Three roots, three jobs. The first maps the field — what the reading-the-box methods actually are. The second runs the experiment — whether a language model tracks what a human knows. The third makes the distinction the whole debate turns on: epistemic opacity — the technical kind, where the box outruns the reading of it — versus intentional opacity — the proprietary kind, where someone chooses to keep the lid on. The box this site studies is the first kind. The second kind is a decision.

Take us to the root → Marcinkevičs & Vogt, “Interpretable and explainable machine learning: A methods-centric overview with concrete examples” (WIREs Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery, 2023; open, CC BY) — the field map: what the methods for reading a model actually are, with worked examples (opens in new tab) Trott, Jones, Chang, Michaelov & Bergen, “Do Large Language Models Know What Humans Know?” (Cognitive Science, 2023; open, CC BY) — where this room meets the other-minds room: an experiment testing whether a language model tracks what a human knows and doesn’t (opens in new tab) Páez, “Explainability of Algorithms” (preprint; free on arXiv) — the chapter that splits opacity in two: epistemic (technical — the box outruns the reading) versus intentional (proprietary — someone keeps the lid on) (opens in new tab) The same chapter of record — Páez, “Explainability of Algorithms,” in A Companion to Digital Ethics (Wiley, 2025; behind the wall) (opens in new tab)

We point; we don’t reproduce. The first two roots are open all the way through. The third is a book chapter behind Wiley’s wall — the author’s preprint above is free and carries the same argument; start there.
